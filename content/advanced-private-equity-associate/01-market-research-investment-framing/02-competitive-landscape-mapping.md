# Competitive Landscape Mapping

## Learning Objectives

- Map competitive landscape systematically across direct, indirect, and adjacent competitors
- Evaluate competitive positioning using frameworks like Porter's Five Forces
- Identify competitive threats and opportunities for differentiation
- Assess competitive intensity and its implications for margins and growth

## Written Guide

### Why Competitive Mapping Matters

- Understanding competitive position relative to peers
- Identifying threats to market share and margins
- Assessing sustainability of competitive advantages

### Identifying Competitors

- Direct competitors (same products, same customers)
- Indirect competitors (different products, same need)
- Adjacent competitors (potential entrants or substitutes)
- Emerging threats (startups, technology disruptors)

### Competitive Positioning Frameworks

- Porter's Five Forces applied to deal context
- Market share and relative scale
- Geographic and product overlap analysis
- Price positioning and value proposition comparison

### Competitive Intelligence Gathering

- Public filings and investor presentations
- Customer and supplier conversations
- Industry conferences and trade publications
- Expert networks and channel checks

### Assessing Competitive Intensity

- Rational vs. irrational pricing behavior
- Switching costs and customer lock-in
- Barriers to entry and exit
- Historical M&A and consolidation trends

### Implications for Investment Thesis

- How competitive dynamics affect margin sustainability
- Defensive moats vs. offensive growth opportunities
- Risk of share loss vs. potential for share gain
- Valuation implications of competitive positioning

## Key Takeaways

- Map all competitors: direct, indirect, adjacent, and emerging threats
- Use frameworks (Porter's Five Forces, market share, positioning) to assess competitive dynamics
- Competitive intelligence comes from public filings, customers, suppliers, experts
- Competitive intensity determines margin sustainability and pricing power
- Strong competitive position justifies higher valuation; weak position requires discounts
