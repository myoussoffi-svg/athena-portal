# Synthesizing VDR Insights

## Outcomes
- Build a work product you could share internally.
- Identify the decisions and risks this work supports.
- Execute a repeatable workflow under time pressure.

## Agenda
- Define the objective and decision context.
- Gather the minimum inputs needed.
- Analyze and pressure-test assumptions.
- Translate findings into implications and next steps.

## Why this matters in a real deal
This work determines whether the deal narrative survives scrutiny. Done well, it improves decision quality and surfaces risk early. Done poorly, it creates false confidence and wastes diligence time.

## Deliverable
- Produce a 1–2 page output (memo, outline, or analysis) that is decision-relevant and defensible.

## Checklist
- [ ] Scope and definitions are explicit.
- [ ] Key assumptions are stated and testable.
- [ ] Downside risks are identified and prioritized.
- [ ] Findings translate into underwriting implications.
- [ ] Next diligence actions are clear.
## Learning Objectives

- Explain how to extract actionable insights from disparate VDR documents rather than simply cataloging what was reviewed
- Evaluate when data points across multiple documents confirm or contradict each other and determine which source is authoritative
- Develop frameworks for synthesizing quantitative data (financials, KPIs) and qualitative context (contracts, board minutes) into unified findings
- Diagnose gaps in available data that prevent definitive conclusions and structure follow-up requests to close those gaps
- Structure synthesis outputs (diligence memos, executive summaries) that communicate findings clearly to VPs, principals, and IC audiences

## Written Guide

### Why Synthesis is the Associate's Highest-Value Activity

Reading documents is table stakes. Synthesizing them into insights is what separates capable associates from those who add strategic value.

Synthesis means:
- Connecting data points across functional areas to test the investment thesis
- Identifying inconsistencies that require reconciliation or escalation
- Translating raw information into implications for valuation, risk, or value creation
- Structuring findings so decision-makers can act without re-reading source documents

Associates who fail to synthesize produce outputs that amount to "data summaries" rather than diligence insights. VPs do not want to know that you read the customer contracts. They want to know what the contracts reveal about retention risk, pricing power, and termination exposure.

### Building a Synthesis Framework: Questions Before Documents

Before diving into document review, establish the questions you are trying to answer. These questions flow from the investment thesis and the key risks flagged during initial screening.

For example:
- **Thesis: Revenue growth through new customer acquisition**
  - Synthesis questions: What is the historical customer acquisition cost trend? How does sales cycle length vary by segment? What percentage of pipeline converts to signed contracts?

- **Thesis: Margin expansion through cost optimization**
  - Synthesis questions: What is the largest cost bucket as a percentage of revenue? Are vendor contracts negotiable or locked in? What is the variability in gross margin by product line?

- **Risk: Customer concentration**
  - Synthesis questions: How long have top customers been with the company? What contractual protections exist against termination? Are there any customers currently in renewal negotiations?

Each question should map to specific documents in the VDR. If no documents exist to answer a critical question, that gap becomes a follow-up priority.

### Cross-Referencing Data: Confirming vs. Contradicting Sources

VDRs often contain multiple documents that touch on the same topic. Your job is to determine:
- Which source is most authoritative
- Whether the sources agree or conflict
- If they conflict, why that conflict exists and what it implies

**Example 1: Revenue figures**
- The management presentation shows $50M in FY23 revenue
- The audited financials show $48M
- The board deck presented in Q4 FY23 projected $52M

**Synthesis approach**:
1. Start with the audited financials as the authoritative source (audited > unaudited)
2. Note the $2M discrepancy between actuals and the management presentation
3. Investigate whether the presentation used a pro forma number (e.g., including an acquisition) or made an error
4. Flag the $4M variance between projection and actuals as a question for management (miss on execution or overly aggressive budget?)

**Example 2: Customer churn**
- The CIM states "best-in-class retention with <5% annual churn"
- The detailed customer revenue data shows 12% revenue churn in FY23

**Synthesis approach**:
1. Identify that "customer churn" (logo loss) and "revenue churn" (dollar loss) are different metrics
2. Clarify with management which metric is being cited and whether net revenue retention (including upsells) differs from gross churn
3. If the discrepancy cannot be explained, flag it as a credibility issue

Do not assume that documents in the VDR are error-free or consistent. Part of synthesis is catching these gaps before they become IC problems.

### Quantitative Synthesis: Trend Analysis and Ratio Testing

Quantitative data (financials, KPIs, operational metrics) should be synthesized into trends, ratios, and benchmarks—not just regurgitated as tables.

**Trend analysis**:
Rather than stating "Revenue was $40M in FY21, $45M in FY22, and $48M in FY23," synthesize as:
- "Revenue grew at a CAGR of 9.5% from FY21–FY23, decelerating from 12.5% in FY22 to 6.7% in FY23, suggesting headwinds in recent periods."

**Ratio testing**:
Calculate and track ratios that reveal operational health:
- Gross margin by product line or customer segment
- CAC payback period
- Revenue per employee
- Days sales outstanding (DSO) and inventory turns

Compare these ratios to:
- Company's own historical performance (is it improving or deteriorating?)
- Industry benchmarks (is this business better or worse than peers?)
- Thesis assumptions (does this support or undermine the thesis?)

**Variance analysis**:
Identify where actual performance diverges from budget or projections:
- If EBITDA was budgeted at $10M but came in at $8M, what drove the miss? Revenue shortfall or cost overrun?
- If working capital burned more cash than expected, was it timing or a structural issue?

These insights inform modeling assumptions, valuation adjustments, and risk assessment.

### Qualitative Synthesis: Contracts, Agreements, and Governance Documents

Qualitative documents (contracts, board minutes, employment agreements) require a different synthesis approach. The goal is to extract:
- **Obligations and constraints**: What is the company required to do or prohibited from doing?
- **Risks and contingencies**: What events could trigger liabilities, terminations, or penalties?
- **Rights and optionality**: What flexibility does the company (or PE buyer) have post-close?

**Customer contracts**:
Do not summarize each contract individually. Instead, synthesize across the portfolio:
- **Terms**: What is the distribution of contract lengths? Are they evergreen, annual, or multi-year?
- **Pricing**: Is pricing fixed or subject to escalators? Are there volume discounts or rebates?
- **Termination rights**: Can customers exit without penalty? What is the notice period?
- **Change of control provisions**: Do any contracts allow termination upon acquisition by a PE firm?

**Vendor agreements**:
- Identify single-source dependencies (vendors that cannot be easily replaced)
- Flag auto-renewal clauses that lock in unfavorable pricing
- Note any exclusivity arrangements that limit operational flexibility

**Board minutes and governance documents**:
- Track major strategic decisions (e.g., entering new markets, discontinuing product lines)
- Identify disagreements or dissents among board members or management
- Flag any discussions of material risks (regulatory investigations, large customer losses, litigation threats)

Synthesize qualitative findings into risk categories (legal, operational, reputational) and assess whether they are deal-breaking, manageable, or immaterial.

### Structuring Synthesis Outputs: Diligence Summaries and Memos

Synthesis is useless if it is not communicated clearly. Associates should produce structured outputs that enable decision-making:

**Functional area summary** (1–2 pages per area):
- **Overview**: What did we review? (List key documents)
- **Key findings**: What did we learn? (3–5 bullets, prioritized by importance)
- **Risks**: What issues were identified? (Categorized by severity: high/medium/low)
- **Open items**: What questions remain unanswered? (Linked to follow-up requests)

**Executive summary** (1 page, for VPs/principals):
- **Thesis confirmation**: Does diligence support or challenge the core thesis?
- **Top risks**: What are the 2–3 issues that could affect valuation or deal structure?
- **Critical gaps**: What information is still missing and when will it be obtained?

**IC-ready synthesis** (integrated into IC memo):
- Findings should be woven into the relevant sections of the IC memo (commercial, financial, operational)
- Each claim should be supported by specific document references (e.g., "per FY23 audited financials, page 12")
- Risks should be quantified where possible (e.g., "If top customer churns, revenue impact is ~$8M or 16% of total")

### Common Synthesis Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Listing facts without interpretation**
Bad: "Customer A has a three-year contract. Customer B has a one-year contract."
Good: "The top two customers (40% of revenue) have contracts expiring within 12 months, creating near-term renewal risk."

**Mistake 2: Ignoring inconsistencies**
If two documents conflict, do not pick one arbitrarily. Flag the inconsistency, hypothesize why it exists, and seek clarification.

**Mistake 3: Over-summarizing**
Synthesis does not mean condensing everything into bullet points. It means extracting what matters and providing enough context for decision-making.

**Mistake 4: Failure to link findings to thesis or valuation**
Every synthesis output should answer: "So what? Why does this matter for the investment decision?"

**Mistake 5: Writing for yourself instead of your audience**
VPs and IC members do not have time to decode dense paragraphs or hunt for conclusions. Lead with the insight, then provide supporting detail.

### Iterative Synthesis: Updating as Diligence Progresses

Synthesis is not a one-time activity. As new documents arrive, follow-up questions are answered, and third-party reports are delivered, your synthesis must be updated.

Maintain version control:
- Date each synthesis output
- Track what has changed since the last version
- Highlight new findings or resolved questions

This iterative approach ensures that by the time you reach IC, your synthesis is comprehensive, current, and defensible.

## Key Takeaways

- Synthesis is about extracting insights and implications, not cataloging what you read—VPs want to know what the data reveals, not just that you reviewed it
- Establish the key questions you are trying to answer before diving into documents, ensuring every synthesis effort is thesis-driven
- Cross-reference data across multiple sources to confirm consistency or identify conflicts, and always determine which source is authoritative
- Synthesize quantitative data into trends, ratios, and variance analysis that reveal operational health and inform modeling assumptions
- Extract obligations, risks, and optionality from qualitative documents (contracts, board minutes) and categorize findings by materiality
- Structure synthesis outputs (functional summaries, executive summaries, IC-ready memos) that communicate findings clearly and link to investment thesis or valuation impact
