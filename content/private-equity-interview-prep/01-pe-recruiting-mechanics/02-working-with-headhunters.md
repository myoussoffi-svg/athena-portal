# Working with Headhunters

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the role of headhunters in PE recruiting and how they operate
- Understand how to get on headhunters' radar and manage initial conversations
- Evaluate how to present your profile and preferences to headhunters effectively
- Identify common mistakes candidates make when working with headhunters

## Written Guide

### The Role of Headhunters in PE Recruiting

**Headhunters** (also called recruiters or search firms) serve as intermediaries between PE funds and candidates. Most top PE firms outsource recruiting to a small number of headhunting firms rather than managing the process internally.

Headhunters:
- Source and screen candidates for PE funds
- Present shortlists of qualified candidates to funds
- Coordinate interview logistics
- Provide feedback (though often limited)

Top PE headhunting firms include CPI, SG Partners (formerly Glocap), Dynamics, Amity, and Oxbridge. A handful of firms dominate mega-fund recruiting.

### How Headhunters Identify Candidates

Headhunters build lists of candidates based on:

**Banking pedigree**: Analysts from bulge bracket or elite boutique banks are prioritized. Group matters less than firm brand, but M&A, coverage, and leveraged finance groups are well-represented.

**Deal experience**: Headhunters review candidates' transaction experience, looking for live deal exposure and technical fluency.

**Performance**: Strong reviews, rankings, or recommendations from senior bankers help. Headhunters often ask for references.

**Networking**: Candidates who proactively reach out to headhunters or are referred by PE professionals get on the radar earlier.

**Geography and timing**: Headhunters target analysts who are in the right location (e.g., New York, San Francisco) and at the right stage of their banking program (typically 6-18 months in).

### Getting on Headhunters' Radar

If you're at a target bank, headhunters will likely find you. However, being proactive helps:

**Reach out early**: Email headhunters in the spring or early summer of Year 1, before recruiting officially begins. Introduce yourself, express interest in PE, and ask to stay in touch.

**Update your resume**: Headhunters will ask for your resume. Make sure it's clean, quantifies your deal experience, and highlights technical work.

**Leverage referrals**: If you know PE professionals who work with a specific headhunter, ask for an introduction. Referrals move you to the top of the list.

**Attend headhunter events**: Some headhunters host networking events for analysts. Attend if invited.

**Be responsive**: When a headhunter calls or emails, respond quickly. Missing an initial outreach can remove you from consideration.

### The Initial Headhunter Call

When a headhunter first contacts you, they're assessing:

**Your interest level**: Are you serious about PE, or just exploring?

**Your readiness**: Can you articulate why PE? Do you have a clear view of what you want?

**Your technical preparedness**: Are you ready to interview, or do you need weeks of prep?

**Your preferences**: What fund sizes, geographies, and sectors interest you?

**Your availability**: Can you interview in the next few weeks?

During this call:

- Be professional and enthusiastic (but not desperate)
- Clearly state your interest in PE and your readiness to interview
- Share your deal experience and technical skills
- Discuss your preferences honestly (fund size, sector, geography)
- Ask about the timeline and what to expect next

Don't oversell or be vague. Headhunters can tell when candidates are unprepared or unsure.

### Managing Your Relationship with Headhunters

**Be honest about your preferences**: If you're only interested in mega-funds, say so. If you're open to middle market, say that too. Headhunters appreciate clarity.

**Don't be difficult**: Headhunters work with dozens of candidates. Being demanding, unresponsive, or entitled harms your chances.

**Understand their incentive structure**: Headhunters are paid by PE funds, not candidates. Their job is to fill roles with strong candidates, but they're not your personal career coach.

**Follow up appropriately**: After interviews, it's fine to check in with the headhunter for feedback. But don't expect real-time updates or detailed explanations.

**Don't work against them**: If a headhunter submits you to a fund, don't try to bypass them and reach out directly. This burns the relationship and can disqualify you.

**Stay organized**: You may work with multiple headhunters simultaneously. Track who submitted you where to avoid conflicts.

### What Headhunters Look For

When headhunters decide whether to present you to a fund, they evaluate:

**Pedigree**: Top bank, strong group, solid school

**Deals**: Live transaction experience, ideally with some closed deals

**Technical skills**: Confident with modeling, valuation, and case studies

**Communication**: Clear, concise, and professional in conversations

**Fit**: Whether your background and interests align with the fund's focus

**Coachability**: Openness to feedback and preparation

Headhunters want candidates who will perform well in interviews and accept offers. They have reputations to protect.

### Common Mistakes

**Ignoring headhunters until they call**: Proactive outreach is better than waiting. Build relationships before recruiting heats up.

**Being vague about your interests**: "I'm open to anything" signals lack of focus. Be thoughtful about what you want.

**Overstating your preparedness**: If you're not ready to interview, say so. Headhunters would rather wait than send an unprepared candidate.

**Burning bridges**: If you get an offer from one headhunter's client and decline, be respectful. You may need to work with them again for future roles.

**Expecting too much feedback**: Headhunters often can't or won't share detailed interview feedback. Don't press too hard.
