## Written Guide

### Why Red Flag Identification is a Core Associate Responsibility

Private equity diligence is not about finding reasons to say no. It is about understanding risks, quantifying them, and determining whether they can be priced or mitigated. However, some risks are severe enough to warrant immediate escalation or deal termination.

Associates are the first line of defense in identifying red flags. You are closest to the data, reviewing documents in detail that VPs and principals may only skim. Missing a red flag—or failing to escalate it promptly—can result in:
- Investing in a business with undisclosed liabilities
- Overpaying for a company with deteriorating fundamentals
- Post-close surprises that destroy value creation plans
- Reputational damage if issues were knowable during diligence

Conversely, over-escalating minor issues as red flags creates noise, erodes credibility, and distracts the deal team from real risks. Associates must develop judgment to distinguish between genuine red flags and normal business complexity.

### Categories of Red Flags

Red flags generally fall into one of five categories:

**1. Financial Red Flags**
Indicators of deteriorating performance, accounting irregularities, or financial misrepresentation:
- Revenue declining or decelerating without clear explanation
- Gross margins compressing with no corresponding cost increase narrative
- Working capital ballooning beyond normal seasonality
- Significant one-time adjustments or "extraordinary items" in recent periods
- Variance between management-reported EBITDA and audited figures
- Frequent restatements or amendments to historical financials
- Unreconciled discrepancies between board reporting and audited statements

**2. Commercial Red Flags**
Indicators of customer concentration risk, competitive pressure, or revenue quality issues:
- Top customer recently reduced spend or terminated relationship
- Major customer contracts expiring in next 6–12 months without visibility on renewal
- Pricing pressure from new entrants or commoditization trends
- High customer churn in recent quarters with no retention initiatives
- Pipeline or backlog declining quarter-over-quarter
- Material revenue coming from customers with poor credit or payment history

**3. Operational Red Flags**
Indicators of execution risk, capacity constraints, or operational dysfunction:
- Key suppliers facing financial distress or supply chain disruptions
- Single-source dependencies with no backup vendors
- Production capacity utilization exceeding 90% with no expansion plans
- High employee turnover in critical roles (engineering, sales leadership)
- Unplanned departures of senior management in past 12 months
- Regulatory violations, safety incidents, or quality control failures

**4. Legal and Compliance Red Flags**
Indicators of undisclosed liabilities, litigation exposure, or governance failures:
- Ongoing or threatened material litigation
- Regulatory investigations or enforcement actions
- Material contracts with change-of-control termination clauses
- Unfunded pension liabilities or employee benefit obligations
- Environmental contamination or remediation requirements
- Tax audits or disputes with tax authorities
- Intellectual property challenges or infringement claims

**5. Credibility and Integrity Red Flags**
Indicators that management or sellers may be misrepresenting the business:
- Information provided contradicts prior statements or CIM representations
- Reluctance or delays in providing standard diligence materials
- Evasive or inconsistent answers to direct questions
- Financial projections disconnected from historical performance with no credible bridge
- Evidence of aggressive accounting (revenue recognition, capitalization of expenses)
- Related-party transactions not disclosed in initial materials

### Evaluating Red Flag Severity: Deal-Breaker vs. Manageable Risk

Not every red flag kills a deal. Your job is to assess severity and determine the appropriate response.

**Deal-Breaker Red Flags** (immediate escalation, potential walk-away):
- Fraud or intentional misrepresentation (e.g., fictitious revenue, hidden liabilities)
- Undisclosed material litigation that could result in catastrophic loss
- Regulatory violations that could lead to license revocation or shutdown
- Existential customer concentration (e.g., 80% of revenue from one customer who is planning to churn)
- Financial deterioration so severe that the thesis is invalidated (e.g., business is cash flow negative with no path to profitability)

**High-Severity Red Flags** (urgent escalation, may affect valuation or structure):
- Customer churn accelerating in recent quarters
- Margin compression driven by competitive dynamics
- Key employee departures with no succession plan
- Working capital requirements materially higher than projected
- Material contracts expiring soon with uncertain renewal terms

**Medium-Severity Red Flags** (escalate with recommended mitigation):
- Single-source vendor dependency (mitigated by securing backup suppliers post-close)
- High customer concentration but with long-term contracts and strong relationships
- Historical compliance issues that have since been remediated
- Operational inefficiencies that represent value creation opportunities

**Low-Severity Red Flags** (document but may not require immediate escalation):
- Minor discrepancies in non-material data
- Isolated customer complaints with no pattern
- Administrative compliance gaps (e.g., missing board minutes for non-critical meetings)

Severity assessment should consider:
- **Magnitude**: How large is the financial or operational impact?
- **Likelihood**: How probable is the risk to materialize?
- **Controllability**: Can the risk be mitigated post-close, or is it outside the firm's control?
- **Timing**: Is this an immediate issue or a future risk?

### Red Flag Escalation Protocol

When you identify a red flag, follow this escalation framework:

**Step 1: Verify the flag**
Before escalating, confirm that the issue is real and not a misunderstanding:
- Cross-check against other documents to rule out data errors
- Review context (e.g., is a revenue decline driven by intentional exit from an unprofitable segment?)
- Assess whether you have all relevant information or if this is a partial view

**Step 2: Assess severity**
Use the framework above to categorize the flag as deal-breaker, high, medium, or low severity.

**Step 3: Draft a clear escalation message**
Your escalation should include:
- **What you found**: Specific description of the issue with document references
- **Why it matters**: Implications for thesis, valuation, or deal structure
- **What is known vs. unknown**: Clarify gaps in information
- **Recommended next steps**: Propose follow-up questions, third-party diligence, or deal team discussion

**Example escalation message**:
"**Red Flag: Customer Concentration Risk**

During review of the customer revenue detail (VDR Folder 3.2), I identified that the top customer (Customer A) represents 42% of FY23 revenue, up from 35% in FY21. Additionally, the contract with Customer A expires in 6 months (June 2024) and is subject to 90-day termination without cause.

**Implications**:
- If Customer A does not renew, the business loses ~$20M in revenue, or approximately 40% of projected FY24 EBITDA.
- The thesis assumes diversified revenue with no single customer >20%.

**Unknown**:
- Status of renewal discussions with Customer A
- Historical renewal rate and pricing dynamics
- Whether there are contractual protections (e.g., minimum volume commitments)

**Recommended Next Steps**:
1. Request management to provide update on Customer A renewal discussions in tomorrow's call
2. Request historical contract renewal terms and any correspondence with Customer A
3. Assess impact on valuation if Customer A revenue is excluded or haircut

Please advise how you would like to proceed."

This format provides actionable information without requiring your VP to re-read the source documents or guess at implications.

**Step 4: Escalate promptly**
- **Deal-breaker or high-severity flags**: Escalate immediately (same day, via call or urgent email)
- **Medium-severity flags**: Escalate within 24 hours
- **Low-severity flags**: Include in next scheduled update or diligence summary

Do not sit on high-severity flags waiting for the "right time" to raise them. The right time is as soon as you have verified the issue.

### Distinguishing Operational Challenges from Misrepresentation

Some red flags reflect normal business challenges (operational issues, competitive pressure, market shifts). Others suggest intentional obfuscation or misrepresentation.

**Operational challenges** (manageable with transparency):
- Management openly discusses underperformance and explains root causes
- Data provided is consistent, even if it reveals weaknesses
- Management has credible action plans to address issues
- Issues are recent and explainable by external factors (e.g., supply chain disruptions, temporary customer loss)

**Misrepresentation** (credibility concern, potential deal-breaker):
- Information provided contradicts CIM or prior statements without acknowledgment
- Management is evasive or changes explanations when pressed
- Adjusted EBITDA includes aggressive add-backs with no supporting documentation
- Key data points are withheld or provided only after repeated requests
- Financial projections are disconnected from reality with no credible assumptions

If you suspect misrepresentation, document the specific instances and escalate immediately. Do not attempt to "give the benefit of the doubt" on credibility issues—this is a decision for your VP or principal.

### Managing False Positives: When Red Flags Resolve

Not every red flag survives deeper investigation. Sometimes what appears alarming at first has a benign explanation:
- A revenue decline was due to intentional exit from a low-margin segment
- A customer "churn" was actually a planned transition to a higher-value contract
- A vendor dependency exists but with long-term locked-in pricing and no alternative needed

When a red flag resolves:
1. **Document the resolution** in your diligence tracker or memo
2. **Update your VP** so they are not caught off-guard if the topic resurfaces
3. **Retain the original concern in your notes** in case new information changes the conclusion

Do not feel embarrassed about raising a flag that later resolves. It is better to escalate and confirm than to miss a real issue.

### Building Red Flag Judgment Over Time

Red flag identification is a learned skill. Early in your career, you may:
- Over-escalate minor issues out of caution
- Under-escalate major issues due to lack of pattern recognition
- Struggle to distinguish noise from signal

Accelerate your learning by:
- Reviewing prior deal post-mortems to see what red flags were missed
- Asking VPs for feedback on your escalations (Were they appropriately prioritized? Was the framing clear?)
- Building a mental library of "what red flags looked like" across deals
- Discussing ambiguous cases with peers or mentors to calibrate judgment

Over time, you will develop intuition for what matters and what does not—and that judgment is one of the most valuable skills a PE associate can build.

## Key Takeaways

- Red flag identification is a core associate responsibility—missing or failing to escalate a material risk can lead to bad investments or post-close value destruction
- Red flags fall into five categories: financial, commercial, operational, legal/compliance, and credibility/integrity—each requires different assessment and response
- Evaluate severity by considering magnitude, likelihood, controllability, and timing—not every red flag kills a deal, but deal-breakers require immediate escalation
- Follow a disciplined escalation protocol: verify the flag, assess severity, draft a clear message with implications and recommended next steps, and escalate promptly
- Distinguish between operational challenges (manageable with transparency and action plans) and misrepresentation (credibility concerns that may be deal-breakers)
- Document resolutions when red flags turn out to be false positives, and build judgment over time by reviewing post-mortems and seeking feedback on escalations
